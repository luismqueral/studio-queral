# what is a "generative strategy"?

<div class="mw6 center tl mb4">

## you don't make the cards — you cultivate them

- <a href="https://strategy.garden" target="_blank" rel="noopener noreferrer">strategy.garden</a> is a deck of 265 lateral thinking cards. you draw one and apply it to whatever you're stuck on. each card gives you a constraint, a reframe, a way of looking at your problem through a completely different domain — woodworking, architecture, fermentation, film editing, medicine, philosophy.

- I mentioned the deck briefly in <a href="/notes/managing-generative-arena">on managing the generative arena</a> — drawing a card is arena management in miniature. the card is the condition, what you think of is the emergence, deciding if it's useful is the feedback, keeping the good ideas is the harvest.

- the obvious ancestor is Brian Eno and Peter Schmidt's Oblique Strategies. "Use an old idea." "Honor thy error as a hidden intention." those are great. but Eno and Schmidt wrote their cards from personal creative practice. they authored aphorisms.

- the cards in this deck aren't authored. they're *extracted*. harvested from source texts, scored against a rubric, rewritten until they speak from inside a domain rather than about it.

- which means the process of making the deck is itself a generative process — the same conditions → emergence → feedback → harvest loop the cards are about. the deck practices the thing it describes. I'm not sure if that's elegant or just circular.

---

## where the cards come from

- the first source texts were Minsky's *Society of Mind* and Papert's *Mindstorms*. both books think in transferable structures. Minsky describes agents, hierarchies, feedback, frames — patterns that show up in codebases and gardens and organizations, not just minds. Papert's ideas about learning-by-making and "objects to think with" are structural, not pedagogical. they apply anywhere someone is building understanding through doing.

- the extraction process: read the text, look for structural patterns that recur across domains, write a card that *embodies* that pattern from inside its source domain, then stress-test it. can you apply this card to a codebase? a medical record? a dataset? a creative brief? if it only works in one context, it's not a strategy card. it's advice.

- from there the domains multiplied. architecture. woodworking. cooking. film. medicine. mathematics. Magic: The Gathering. each domain brings a different texture. the cooking cards feel like mise en place. the architecture cards feel like walking through buildings. the games cards feel like sitting across from an opponent.

- the deck now has 265 cards across 12 themes: Philosophy (46), Science (38), History (31), Art (28), Nature (21), Psychology (20), Architecture (20), Cooking & Making (20), Experimental (15), Games (10), Politics (9), Religion (7).

- the surprise was which domains produced the strongest cards. architecture and craft averaged 16.2 on the rubric. philosophy averaged 12.0. the more concrete and sensory the source, the better the card. I assumed philosophy — the discipline literally about thinking — would produce the best lateral thinking prompts. it didn't. the cards that *feel* like something outperform the cards that *explain* something.

- next up for extraction: Bateson's *Steps to an Ecology of Mind*, the Zhuangzi, and Deleuze & Guattari's *A Thousand Plateaus*. Bateson is exciting because every concept in that book already bridges domains — he worked across anthropology, cybernetics, psychiatry, and ecology. the Zhuangzi is exciting because it's already a collection of parables. Cook Ding butchering the ox, the useless tree, the empty boat. each one is a strategy card waiting to be harvested.

## the rubric as constraint

- every card gets scored on six dimensions. 1–3 each, max 18.

- **voice & embodiment** — does the card speak *from within* its source domain, or merely *about* it? a 3 inhabits the experience. a 1 opens with "Apply X's concept of Y."

- **surprise potential** — would this card genuinely derail someone's thinking? a 3 forces a cognitive shift. a 1 confirms existing intuitions through a thin metaphor.

- **interdisciplinary bridge** — does the connection carry weight in both directions? a 3 teaches you something about the source domain *and* about your problem. a 1 means the domain is decorative.

- **specificity of source** — does the card teach you something real about dovetail joints, section cuts, or fermentation? or could the source be swapped without losing anything?

- **openness of application** — does the card produce genuine insight when applied to a codebase, a dataset, a medical record, *and* a creative brief? the test is three or more radically different substrates.

- **closing pull** — does the ending plant something that keeps working on you? or does it end with "detail how this could transform your thinking"?

- the rubric is the constraint in the generative loop. without it, you'd collect interesting ideas. with it, you're filtering for a specific *kind* of card.

- concrete example. "Let the Shavings Tell the Story" scores 18 — perfect across the board:

  > *A curl of wood from a well-tuned plane tells everything: the sharpness of the blade, the grain direction, the moisture content. Every process leaves similar telltale signs. What subtle indicators are you overlooking? What stories are your failures trying to tell you? What secrets might be revealed in the parts you usually sweep away?*

- you learn something real about hand planes. you feel the workshop. and the closing question — *what secrets might be revealed in the parts you usually sweep away?* — lands because you've been inside the domain for three sentences. you're already thinking about shavings when the reframe hits.

- contrast that with "Apply Quantum Tunneling," which scored 11:

  > *Consider this situation through quantum tunneling: particles passing through impenetrable barriers. What supposedly insurmountable obstacles might be traversed by probability, not force? Detail how quantum physics could reveal pathways classical thinking declares impossible.*

- "consider this situation through quantum tunneling" is a lecture, not an experience. you don't learn anything about quantum physics. "detail how" is a homework prompt. the card talks *about* its domain instead of putting you *in* it.

- the audit found this pattern 32 times — 17% of the deck opened with "Apply X's concept of Y." that one anti-pattern accounts for most cards scoring 10–11. the fix is always the same: cut the explanation, start inside the experience, end with an image instead of a directive.

- "Invoke Dadaist Disruption" scored 10. orderly card about disorder. the rewrite, "Commit a Beautiful Nonsense," starts like this:

  > *Take the most orderly part of this situation. Now sabotage it. Gently.*

- no mention of Dada. no theorist. you're already inside the act of sabotage before you know what framework you're using. the card does what it's about.

- the rubric doesn't make cards good. it makes the filtering legible. you can say *why* something doesn't work, which means you can fix it, which means each extraction cycle gets tighter.
